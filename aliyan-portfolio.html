The one that kept surprising me

Catching a fraud signal before Brussels did

Green Line Commodities · Renewable fuels · 2022 to present

Since 2022 I've built the supply and demand model behind Green Line Commodities' renewable fuels desk. It tracks Europe's HVO market and Germany's THG certificate market quarter by quarter, blending paid pricing data with public regulatory registries.

  • I built a fraud check by comparing reported used cooking oil imports against a hard physical ceiling for how much waste oil actually exists in Europe. When that gap crossed 20 percent in late 2023, something was clearly off.
  • That flag came months before the EU opened its own anti dumping investigation into Chinese biodiesel imports.
  • I also modeled a 2027 surplus clock. Certificate volumes banked during the 2025 to 2026 banking ban don't disappear, they re enter the market in 2027, and the desk hadn't priced that in yet.
  • A price decomposition regression separates the green premium from feedstock cost across HVO, gasoil and THG certificates.

The scenario nobody asked for, but I ran anyway

What happens if the Strait of Hormuz closes for 40 days

Independent research project

This one started as a question I couldn't stop thinking about. If the Strait of Hormuz closed for 40 days, what would actually happen to a Pakistani urea trading book?

  • I modeled three things happening at once: gas curtailment cutting urea output at Fauji, Engro, Fatima and Pakarab, ships rerouting around the Cape of Good Hope at 14 extra transit days with a war risk premium, and regional crop yield sensitivity to nutrient shortfalls in Sindh and Punjab.
  • I built a live inventory timeline so I could see exactly when the buffer stock would run out.
  • Then I ran a Monte Carlo simulation of trading margin outcomes across randomized blockade lengths and price paths.
  • The result surprised me. Almost every simulated outcome landed in a loss. A simple long urea, short wheat hedge does not protect margin through a full closure.

The forecast I was honest about

Telling the desk where the model wasn't sure

Louis Dreyfus Company · Cotton research · July 2025

I applied a seasonal ARIMA model to nine years of cotton basis data, from 2016 to 2025, to see if I could forecast where basis was heading next.

  • The model found a real seasonal pattern. A high basis in a given month tends to reverse in that same month a year later.
  • It forecast a seasonal low landing in July, around 400 basis points.
  • I flagged the confidence intervals honestly instead of overselling the number. I told the desk plainly that if we wanted to carry this forward, it needed more research.
  • That note went out as a structured wire to our Pakistan cotton research team, written for traders, not statisticians.

The one that started with wet boots

Measuring a flood before the balance sheet needed a number

Louis Dreyfus Company · Punjab cotton desk · September 2025

"A flood doesn't show up cleanly in a spreadsheet. You have to go and measure it."

In September 2025 I helped run a 284 sample yield survey across Punjab, covering Bahawalpur, Multan and Dera Ghazi Khan. Then the rains came. Two tehsils, Alipur and Jalalpur Peer Wala, went mostly underwater during peak season.

  • I mapped flood extent by tehsil and weighted it against how much of that tehsil actually grows cotton, rather than assuming the whole district was hit equally.
  • I cross checked our farmer reported yields against public harvest pace data, so we weren't relying on a single source during a chaotic season.
  • Behind this survey sit two datasets I keep up year round: daily storage levels across Indus Basin reservoirs including Tarbela, since irrigation availability signals planting decisions before a single survey comes back, and a benchmark of how quickly cotton arrives at market compared with the last fifteen seasons.
  • The adjusted yield estimate was incorporated into the desk's global balance sheet for the season.
